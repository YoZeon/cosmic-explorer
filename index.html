Hubble’s Law & the Expanding Universe

Galaxies are moving away from each other — and the farther away they are, the faster they recede. This simple relationship, discovered by Edwin Hubble and anticipated by Georges Lemaître, reshaped cosmology.

Definition

What is Hubble’s Law?

Hubble’s Law states that a galaxy’s recession velocity (v) is proportional to its distance (d) from us: v = H0 · d, where H0 is the Hubble constant. In plain language: distant galaxies appear to move away faster than nearby ones. This is best explained by space itself expanding, not galaxies flying through a fixed space.

  • Discovered: 1929 analysis by Edwin Hubble (building on earlier redshift work by Vesto Slipher and theory by Georges Lemaître).
  • Key idea: Redshift of light from distant galaxies increases with distance.
  • Implication: The Universe had a beginning and has been expanding since — consistent with the Big Bang Theory.
Evidence for an Expanding Universe

Three Pillars of Evidence

1) Big Bang Theory

The Universe began hot and dense and has been expanding and cooling ever since. Galaxies and large‑scale structure formed as matter clumped under gravity.

2) Redshift of Galaxies

Light from distant galaxies is stretched to longer (redder) wavelengths as space expands. More distant galaxies show larger redshift — exactly what Hubble’s Law predicts.

3) Cosmic Microwave Background (CMBR)

A faint, nearly uniform glow at microwave wavelengths — the afterglow of the hot early Universe — mapped precisely by missions like WMAP and Planck.

What the Evidence Means

  • Space expands uniformly on large scales.
  • Looking farther away is looking back in time.
  • Models including dark matter and dark energy best fit observations.
People

Key Figures

Edwin Hubble (1889–1953) correlated galaxy distances with their redshifts, quantifying the expansion. Georges Lemaître (1894–1966) independently derived the expansion from General Relativity and proposed the “primeval atom” (an early form of the Big Bang idea).

  • Milky Way ≠ the whole Universe: Hubble also showed “spiral nebulae” are external galaxies.
  • Why it matters: Shifted cosmology from a static to a dynamic Universe.
Today’s Picture

Modern Cosmology

WMAP & Planck

Missions like WMAP and Planck mapped the CMB in detail, pinning down the age, composition, and geometry of the Universe and refining the Hubble constant.

Dark Energy

Observations of distant supernovae and the CMB indicate the expansion is accelerating, driven by an unknown component dubbed dark energy.

Why It Matters

  • Explains the Universe’s past and hints at its future (continued acceleration vs. other scenarios).
  • Connects cosmology and astrophysics across observations and theory.
  • Guides ongoing surveys and telescopes studying structure, dark matter, and gravity.
